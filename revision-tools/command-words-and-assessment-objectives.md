# Command Words & Assessment Objectives — Decode Every Question

The single biggest source of dropped marks is **answering the wrong question** — writing a description when the examiner wanted an evaluation, or stating a fact when they wanted you to *apply* it. This page teaches you to read the command word, spot the AO, and pitch your answer at the right level.

---

## The three Assessment Objectives

| AO | The verb in your head | What the examiner rewards | Typical command words |
|----|----------------------|---------------------------|-----------------------|
| **AO1** – Knowledge & understanding | "State / explain what it *is*" | Correct, precise recall of concepts | State, Define, Describe, Identify, Give, Outline |
| **AO2** – Apply | "Use it *in this scenario*" | Linking knowledge to the specific context in the question | Apply, Calculate, Complete, Show how, Explain why (in context), Trace |
| **AO3** – Design, program & evaluate | "Judge / weigh up / build" | Reasoned judgement, comparison, justified conclusions, working solutions | Evaluate, Discuss, Justify, Compare, Analyse, Design, Write a program |

> 💡 **Where grades are decided:** AO1 marks are the "easy" marks almost everyone gets. The **A/A\* boundary lives in AO2 and AO3** — applying ideas to unfamiliar scenarios and producing balanced, justified judgements. Spend your *practice* time disproportionately on AO2/AO3 questions.

## Command-word dictionary (what each one actually demands)

| Command word | What you must do | Trap to avoid |
|--------------|------------------|---------------|
| **State / Give / Identify** | One short, precise point. No explanation needed. | Wasting time writing a paragraph for 1 mark. |
| **Define** | A precise, exam-ready definition. | Vague, circular wording ("encryption is when you encrypt"). |
| **Describe** | Say *what* something is / *what happens* — a sequence of factual points. | Forgetting it needs detail; one word ≠ a description. |
| **Explain** | Give a reason — a point **plus** *why/how* (cause → effect). Look for "so that…", "which means…". | Listing facts without the "because". |
| **Explain why / how (in context)** | AO2 — link the concept to the scenario named in the question. | Generic textbook answer that ignores the scenario. |
| **Compare** | Discuss **both** things together, ideally point-by-point, with explicit *similarities and differences* ("whereas", "in contrast"). | Writing two separate descriptions and never linking them. |
| **Discuss** | Extended response — explore multiple sides/stakeholders, benefits **and** drawbacks. | One-sided answer; no breadth. |
| **Evaluate** | Weigh strengths against weaknesses **and reach a justified conclusion**. | Listing pros/cons but never concluding. |
| **Justify** | Give reasons that *support a choice* and say why alternatives are weaker. | Stating the choice without defending it. |
| **Analyse** | Break a problem/scenario into parts and examine how they relate. | Surface-level description. |
| **Calculate / Complete / Trace** | Show **working**; fill in tables/trace tables step by step. | Giving only a final answer — method marks are lost. |
| **Write / Complete the algorithm** | Produce correct, runnable logic (OCR pseudocode or your language). | Vague English; missing edge cases/initialisation. |

## How to read tariff (marks) → length and depth

- **1–2 marks:** usually AO1. One precise point each. Don't over-write.
- **3–4 marks:** often AO2. Make distinct points *and* link them to the scenario. A "describe" needs ~ that many factual points; an "explain" needs point + reason.
- **6–8 marks:** structured response. Plan 3–4 developed points. Use connectives (because, therefore, which means).
- **9–12 marks:** **levels-of-response / extended answer** (especially section 1.5, and "discuss/evaluate" in 02). Marked in **bands**, not point-per-mark. See below.

---

## Cracking levels-of-response (the 9–12 markers)

These are marked holistically against **Level descriptors**, typically:

| Level | Roughly | What it looks like |
|-------|---------|--------------------|
| **Level 3** (top) | Top marks | Balanced, **well-developed** discussion of *several* factors/stakeholders; clear, logical structure; a **justified conclusion**; specialist terminology used accurately. |
| **Level 2** (mid) | Middle | Some development and some balance, but one-sided or thin in places; reasoning partially linked; conclusion weak or missing. |
| **Level 1** (low) | Few marks | Fragmented points, mostly description/assertion, little or no development, no real conclusion. |

**To hit Level 3 every time, use this skeleton:**
1. **Define / set the scene** (1 sentence).
2. **Argument FOR** — a benefit, *developed* (point → because → consequence), linked to a stakeholder.
3. **Argument AGAINST** — a drawback, equally developed, ideally a *different* stakeholder.
4. **Repeat** for a second pair of points (breadth = more stakeholders/angles: legal, ethical, economic, environmental, social, technical).
5. **Justified conclusion** — make a judgement and say *why*, acknowledging the trade-off.

> 💡 **A\* habit:** name **specific stakeholders** (employees, customers, businesses, government, society, the environment) and bring in **multiple perspectives** (ethical *and* legal *and* economic). Breadth + balance + a defended conclusion = top band.

## 30-second pre-answer checklist (do this on every question)

1. **Circle the command word.** What is it actually asking — recall, apply, or judge?
2. **Note the tariff.** How many distinct/developed points do I need?
3. **Underline the scenario/context.** If it's AO2, my answer must mention it.
4. **For "explain", write the "because".** For "compare", write the "whereas". For "evaluate", write the "overall…".
5. **Show working** on anything numeric or traced.

Get this right and you stop throwing away the marks you actually know.
