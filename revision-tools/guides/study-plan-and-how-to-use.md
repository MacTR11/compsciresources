# How to Use These Resources + Spaced-Recap Study Plan

Revision that works is **active and spaced**, not re-reading until it feels familiar (familiarity is not the same as recall). This page tells you exactly how to use the hub and gives you a ready-made plan.

---

## The method: Recap → Retrieve → Apply → Review

For **every** topic:

1. **Recap (read once, actively).** Read the topic file. As you go, turn each heading into a question ("How does the FDE cycle work?").
2. **Retrieve (close the book).** Write down everything you remember — definitions, diagrams, steps. *This* is what builds memory. Then check and fill gaps in a different colour.
3. **Apply (closed-book questions).** Do the topic's exam-style questions without notes. Mark yourself honestly against the mark scheme.
4. **Review (spaced).** Re-test the topic after **1 day → 1 week → 1 month**. Each successful recall makes it stick longer.

> 💡 **A\* habit:** after marking a question, write *why* you lost each mark in one line ("didn't link to scenario", "forgot edge case"). Re-reading your own error log before the exam is worth more than re-reading notes.

---

## Use the RAG tracker as your command centre

Open [`self-assessment-tracker.md`](self-assessment-tracker.md) and rate every sub-topic:

- 🔴 **Red** — can't do it. **Priority.**
- 🟠 **Amber** — shaky / needs practice.
- 🟢 **Green** — "I could teach this and answer an unseen application question."

Revise **Red and Amber first**, and only mark something Green once you've scored well on its exam questions *closed-book*. Re-RAG after every past paper.

---

## Interleaving — don't block-revise

Studying one topic for three days straight feels productive but recall is weaker than **mixing topics**. Each session, mix:
- one **Component 01** knowledge topic,
- one **Component 02** skills topic (algorithms/programming),
- a few questions from an **earlier** topic you "finished" (spacing).

---

## A sample 8-week countdown plan

Adjust to your exam dates and your RAG ratings (spend longer on Reds). Each "session" ≈ 45–60 min.

| Week | Focus (Component 01) | Focus (Component 02) | Weekend |
|------|----------------------|----------------------|---------|
| 1 | 1.1 Processors & devices | 2.1 Computational thinking | Re-test wk1; 1 past-paper section |
| 2 | 1.2 Software & development | 2.2 Programming techniques | Re-test wk1–2 |
| 3 | 1.3 Exchanging data (networks/db) | 2.2 Computational methods + recursion/OOP | Re-test wk1–3 |
| 4 | 1.4.1 Number representation (drill) | 2.3 Big O + searching/sorting | Mixed-topic question set |
| 5 | 1.4.2/1.4.3 Data structures & Boolean | 2.3 Dijkstra/A* + tree traversals | Full past paper (01) timed |
| 6 | 1.5 Legal/ethical (extended response) | NEA polish (if applicable) | Full past paper (02) timed |
| 7 | **Reds & Ambers only** (from RAG) | **Reds & Ambers only** | Past paper + mark vs scheme |
| 8 | Past papers, error-log review, glossary, common-mistakes sheet | as left | Light recap, rest before exam |

> Two written papers ≈ **40% each**; the NEA is **20%** and is usually completed earlier in the year. If your project is still open, protect time for it — it's 70 "easier" marks if you follow the [NEA guide](../../component-03-04-programming-project/README.md).

## Weekly checklist

- [ ] Re-tested last week's topics from memory (not re-read)
- [ ] Did at least one set of exam-style questions **closed-book** and marked it
- [ ] Updated my **RAG tracker**
- [ ] Added this week's mistakes to my **error log**
- [ ] Interleaved an older topic
- [ ] (Within ~4 weeks of exams) at least one **timed** past-paper section

---

## Tools to lean on
- **Glossary** — for daily quick-fire definition retrieval.
- **Reference sheet** — for the number/Big O/logic/SQL facts you must have at your fingertips.
- **Command-words guide** — read it before every practice session until decoding questions is automatic.
- **Common-mistakes sheet** — read the week of the exam.

Consistency beats cramming. Short, active, spaced sessions — every week — is how passes become A\*s.
